### 🏁 Where It All Started

My journey into cybersecurity began long before I ever touched a CTF or Hack The Box machine.  
Back in high school, I studied **Software Programming**, earning a **Technical Diploma in Software Development**.  
That background gave me a strong foundation in logic, scripting, and how applications are really built something that later became crucial for understanding how to break them.

---

### 🎓 From Student to Certified Professional

After high school, I continued my studies at the **Instituto Tecnológico de las Américas (ITLA)**, where I pursued an **Associate in Science: Cybersecurity Technologist** degree.  
This was where I started connecting the dots between theory and practice.

During that time, I landed an **internship with a cybersecurity company based in Boston**.  
At first, my main role was content development and QA for cybersecurity training materials,but being exposed to that professional environment made me realize what I truly wanted: to **focus on offensive security**.

---

### 🔥 Discovering Hack The Box

While interning, I had already done a few **Hack The Box (HTB)** machines, but I wasn’t yet fully into it.  
Then something clicked I decided to go all-in on the **Red Team path**.

I got the **HTB VIP plan** and started doing machines daily: 8 hours a day straight, exploring techniques, reading writeups, comparing my approaches, and learning from others.  
That’s when my passion for offensive security really took off.

---

### 💼 From Curiosity to Commitment

Almost a year passed, and I decided it was time to test myself with something more official — the **OSCP certification**.  
Using the savings I had from my internship and a few small bounties, I paid for the course.

By then, I had already pwned around **290 HTB machines** and about **60 PG labs**.  
So when I started reviewing the OSCP material, I realized much of it was already familiar.  
I continued studying it while also diving deeper into **malware development** and **Windows internals**, balancing both worlds — practical exploitation and low-level engineering.

---

### 🧠 Passing the OSCP

After 2 months going through the material and doing challenge labs, I took the OSCP exam and **passed on my first attempt with 100%**.  
It was intense, but the real test wasn’t the exam — it was the consistency and curiosity that built up over years of self-learning, breaking things, and rebuilding them better.
> Fun fact: I discovered a unintended path that almost jump half of the Skylark lab.
{: .prompt-tip}
---

### ⚙️ Evolving: Building a Rust C2 Framework

After earning the OSCP, I wanted a project that combined everything I had learned — programming, malware development, and red teaming.  
That’s when I started creating my **C2 Framework in Rust**.

This project includes:
- **Multi-protocol session handlers** (HTTP, gRPC, TCP)
- **COFF loading** with Beacon API support
- **Embedded scripting (Rhai)**
- **File transfer and background job management**

I wanted to create something that reflected the maturity of existing C2s — but with my own design philosophy and Rust’s safety and performance advantages.

---

### 🚀 What’s Next

Now, I’m focusing on growing as a professional — looking for opportunities to **collaborate, learn from experienced operators, and contribute to real-world projects**.  
I’ll also be a **speaker at HackCon2026RD**, leading a **Rust Offensive Tooling Development workshop**, where I’ll share the lessons and technical approaches I’ve learned throughout this journey.

---

> “The OSCP was never the end goal — it was just another step toward mastering the craft.”

---

### 🧩 Final Thoughts

Everyone’s OSCP journey is different, but what really matters is **consistency, curiosity, and creativity**.  
For me, it wasn’t just about getting the cert — it was about proving to myself that years of effort, late nights, and deep dives into code all led somewhere meaningful.

If you’re on this path, **keep breaking, keep learning, and build something that reflects your journey**.
